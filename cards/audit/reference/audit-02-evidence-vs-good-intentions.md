# Evidence vs Good Intentions

## Purpose

To explain why good intentions, verbal assurances, and planned actions are not sufficient in an audit — and to describe what genuine, demonstrable evidence looks like.

## Plain English explanation

In an audit, what you intend to do, what you believe is happening, and what you think is understood carry very little weight on their own. What carries weight is what you can show.

Evidence is anything that demonstrates a control is defined, operating, and maintained. It is tangible. It can be pointed to, reviewed, and verified independently. It does not require the auditor to trust your word.

Good intentions sound like:
- "We plan to review that."
- "Everyone understands the policy."
- "We always check that before deployment."
- "That is being worked on."

Evidence looks like:
- An approved policy with a version number, date, and named owner
- Meeting minutes recording that the policy was reviewed and no changes were needed
- A completed checklist showing pre-deployment checks were carried out
- A corrective action log showing what was found, what was decided, and what was done

The gap between intention and evidence is where audit findings are born.

## Why it matters

Auditors must be able to verify what they are told. If a control cannot be evidenced, an auditor has no basis to confirm it is operating. The result is typically a nonconformity or an observation — regardless of how committed the organisation genuinely is to responsible AI.

Evidence also protects the organisation. When something goes wrong with an AI system, clear records of governance decisions, reviews, and approvals demonstrate that the organisation acted responsibly. Without records, good practice is invisible.

## Key points to cover

- Intent and belief are not evidence
- Evidence must be tangible, retrievable, and independently verifiable
- The most common forms of evidence: approved documents, records, logs, meeting notes, sign-off trails, training records, review outputs, and corrective action records
- Evidence must be current — an outdated document is weak evidence of current practice
- Evidence must be linked to the control it supports — a general policy does not evidence a specific operating control

## Practical examples

**Weak (intention):** "Our team knows they need to assess AI risks before deployment."
**Strong (evidence):** A completed impact assessment form, dated, signed by the responsible person, referencing the specific system and deployment date.

**Weak (intention):** "We have a supplier clause about AI."
**Strong (evidence):** A signed contract or supplier addendum including specific AI-related obligations, plus a record of how compliance with those obligations is monitored.

**Weak (intention):** "Leadership is aware of our AI management approach."
**Strong (evidence):** Board or senior leadership meeting minutes recording a discussion of the AI management system, with decisions noted and actions tracked.

**Weak (intention):** "We communicate the AI policy to staff."
**Strong (evidence):** Training records, induction logs, or a distribution list showing who received the policy, when, and in what format.

## Common misunderstanding

A common assumption is that if a process is well-understood internally, that is sufficient. It is not. Internal understanding is a necessary condition for a control to work, but it is not evidence that it is working. The organisation must be able to show the auditor, not just tell them.

## Suggested memory hook

**Intent is invisible. Evidence is everything.**
If you cannot point to it, it does not exist in the audit room.

## Notes for visual treatment

**Professional card:** A two-column comparison table. Left column: "Good Intentions" with examples of verbal or planned statements. Right column: "Evidence" with the corresponding tangible record. Use clear icons — perhaps a speech bubble for intentions and a document/stamp icon for evidence. Colour-code: amber for intentions, green for evidence.

**Funny card:** Two characters side by side. One is confidently explaining something with a speech bubble full of words — no paper in hand. The other is handing over a neatly labelled folder with a checklist, a stamp, and a date. The auditor is looking at the folder, not the speaker. Caption: "They said it was fine. The folder proved it."
