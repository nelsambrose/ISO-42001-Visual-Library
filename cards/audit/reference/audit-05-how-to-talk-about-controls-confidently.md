# How to Talk About Controls Confidently

## Purpose

To help practitioners communicate clearly and honestly about controls during an audit conversation — covering what the control is, who owns it, how it operates, what evidence exists, and how it is reviewed and improved.

## Plain English explanation

An audit is partly a document review and partly a conversation. Auditors will speak to people across the organisation, not just the compliance lead. Anyone involved in operating a control may be asked about it.

Confident, honest communication does not mean rehearsed scripts or overclaiming. It means being able to answer a small set of straightforward questions clearly, calmly, and with reference to real evidence.

The five things an auditor is likely to ask about any control:

1. **What is this control?** — Can you describe what it is and what it is designed to do?
2. **Who is responsible?** — Who owns this, and what does that ownership involve?
3. **How does it operate?** — What actually happens, step by step, in practice?
4. **What evidence do you have?** — Can you point to records that demonstrate it is running?
5. **How is it reviewed and improved?** — How do you know it is working, and what do you do when it is not?

## Why it matters

Uncertainty or inconsistency in audit conversations raises flags. When different people give different answers about the same control, an auditor will investigate further. When someone overclaims — asserting more than the evidence supports — the credibility of all their evidence weakens.

Calm, honest, evidence-based answers — even when those answers include "we are working on that" — are more effective than confident-sounding descriptions that fall apart when the evidence is requested.

## Key points to cover

- Know the control before the conversation — read the relevant documentation
- Know where the evidence is — be able to retrieve it quickly
- Speak about what actually happens, not what is supposed to happen
- If you do not know something, say so — "I would need to check that" is a legitimate answer
- Do not fill gaps in evidence with explanation — note the gap and explain what is being done about it
- Be consistent — what you say must align with what colleagues say and what the documents show

## Practical examples

### Example: Talking about an AI policy (A.2)

**Auditor:** Can you tell me about your AI policy?

**Weak response:** "Yes, we have an AI policy. It covers all the main areas — ethics, data, responsibility. Everyone is expected to follow it."

**Strong response:** "Yes. The AI policy was approved by our [role] in [month/year]. It is reviewed annually — the last review was in [month/year] and no substantive changes were made. It is stored on our intranet and was communicated to all relevant staff via [method]. The policy owner is [name/role]. I can pull up the policy and the last review record if that would be helpful."

---

### Example: Talking about impact assessment (A.5)

**Auditor:** How does your organisation assess the impact of AI systems?

**Weak response:** "We do an impact assessment before we deploy anything. We consider the risks and make sure we are comfortable before going live."

**Strong response:** "We have a documented impact assessment process. Each new AI system — or significant change to an existing one — triggers an assessment. The accountable person signs off the completed form before deployment. We can walk through an example: [specific system]. The assessment was completed in [month/year], it identified [issue], and we put [action] in place to address it before go-live."

---

### Example: When the evidence is not strong

**Auditor:** Can you show me the review records for this control?

**Honest response:** "The review records for this period are incomplete — we identified this in our internal review last month. We have put a corrective action in place: we are establishing a formal review log by [date]. I can show you the corrective action record if that would be useful."

This is far better than presenting weak or unrelated documents as substitutes for missing evidence.

## Common misunderstanding

Many people believe that admitting a gap or uncertainty will automatically result in a finding. It will not, by itself. What results in a finding is a genuine gap in the management system. Admitting it clearly and showing that it is being addressed is evidence that the management system is working — specifically the improvement cycle.

Overclaiming, by contrast, creates credibility problems. If an auditor cannot verify what you have asserted, every other assertion you make becomes suspect.

## Suggested memory hook

**What is it. Who owns it. How it runs. What proves it. How it improves.**
Five questions. Know the answers. Point to the evidence.

## Notes for visual treatment

**Professional card:** A structured framework card showing the five audit conversation questions as a vertical list, each with a one-line prompt for what a good answer includes. Use a clean, interview-style layout — perhaps a question mark icon next to each prompt. Include a brief note at the bottom: "If the evidence is not there yet, say so clearly and show what is being done about it."

**Funny card:** A person at a desk being interviewed by an auditor. Speech bubbles show the five questions. The interviewee is calm, has a folder open, and is pointing at specific pages. Contrast with a second character (speech bubble only, no folder) who is sweating and talking in circles. Caption: "Five questions. Know the answers. Bring the folder."
