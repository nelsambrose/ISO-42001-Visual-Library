# What an Auditor Actually Looks For in ISO 42001

## Purpose

To explain the auditor mindset: what an auditor is assessing, how they think about evidence, and what they are trying to establish when they examine an AI management system.

## Plain English explanation

An auditor is not trying to catch you out. They are trying to answer one question: does this organisation actually manage AI the way it says it does?

To answer that, they look at five things for every area they examine:

1. **Defined** — Is there a documented approach? Does the organisation know what it is supposed to do?
2. **Implemented** — Is it actually happening, not just written down?
3. **Maintained** — Is it kept up to date? Has it been reviewed?
4. **Reviewed** — Has the organisation checked whether it is working?
5. **Improved** — When issues arise, does the organisation act on them?

A policy that exists but has never been communicated, reviewed, or acted on will not satisfy an auditor. A control that someone can explain clearly, point to evidence for, and describe how it is monitored — will.

## Why it matters

Understanding the auditor's perspective helps you prepare honest, complete evidence rather than scrambling to produce documents at the last moment. It also helps you recognise where genuine gaps exist, so they can be addressed before the audit rather than discovered during it.

## Key points to cover

- Auditors assess the entire lifecycle of a control, not just whether it exists
- Ownership matters — someone must be accountable, and that person must know they are
- Operation matters — the control must be running, not just documented
- Records matter — decisions, reviews, approvals, and exceptions must leave a trail
- Improvement matters — the organisation must show it responds to findings and issues

## Practical examples

**Policy (A.2):** The auditor does not just ask "do you have an AI policy?". They ask: who approved it, when was it last reviewed, how was it communicated, who is accountable for it, and can you show that?

**Impact assessment (A.5):** The auditor asks: how does the organisation assess AI system impacts, who does it, when, and what does the output look like? Saying "we think about it" is not enough.

**Third-party relationships (A.10):** The auditor asks: how do you manage AI-related obligations with suppliers and customers? Are responsibilities documented? Are there records of supplier assessments or contractual requirements?

## Common misunderstanding

Many people assume that having documentation is the same as having a control. It is not. Documentation is the starting point. The auditor also needs to see that the control is understood by those responsible for it, that it is operating in practice, and that there is a record of that operation.

## Suggested memory hook

**Defined. Implemented. Maintained. Reviewed. Improved.**
That is what an auditor is checking — for every control, every time.

## Notes for visual treatment

**Professional card:** A structured diagram or checklist showing the five audit dimensions (Defined, Implemented, Maintained, Reviewed, Improved) applied to a single example control. Clean, corporate style. Could use a traffic-light or maturity scale to show what "not evidenced", "partial", and "fully evidenced" looks like.

**Funny card:** An auditor character (detective or inspector archetype) with a magnifying glass, examining not just a document but a full trail — from the policy on the wall to the meeting notes in the filing cabinet to the person who can actually explain what they do. Caption could be something like: "It's not what you wrote. It's what you did."
