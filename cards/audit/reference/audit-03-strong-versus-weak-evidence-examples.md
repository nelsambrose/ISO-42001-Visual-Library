# Strong Versus Weak Evidence Examples

## Purpose

To give practical, grounded examples of what weak, better, and strong evidence looks like across the key Annex A control areas — so that practitioners can assess and improve the quality of their own evidence before an audit.

## Plain English explanation

Not all evidence is equal. A document that exists but has not been reviewed in three years is weaker than one reviewed last quarter. A policy with no named owner is weaker than one with clear accountability. A log with no decisions recorded is weaker than one that shows what was considered and why.

Auditors distinguish between evidence that merely suggests a control might exist and evidence that demonstrates it is actively managed. The difference between weak and strong evidence is usually:

- **Currency** — is it recent and relevant?
- **Specificity** — does it relate to the actual control being assessed?
- **Completeness** — does it show the full picture (decision, action, review)?
- **Ownership** — is it clear who is responsible?

## Why it matters

Practitioners often overestimate the strength of their evidence because they are close to the work and know what is happening. An auditor sees only what is presented. Building an evidence portfolio means thinking like an auditor — asking "what does this actually prove?" rather than "we know we do this."

## Key points to cover

- Evidence exists on a spectrum from weak to strong
- Weak evidence creates doubt; strong evidence removes it
- The goal is not to produce more documents, but to ensure documents are current, owned, and linked to the control they support
- Evidence should be retrievable quickly — if you cannot find it, it has no audit value at that moment

## Practical examples

### AI policies (A.2)

| Strength | Example |
|---|---|
| Weak | A policy document with no version number, no date, and no named approver |
| Better | A policy document that is dated, approved, and stored centrally |
| Strong | A dated, approved policy with a named owner, a review log showing it was assessed last quarter, and a distribution record showing it was communicated to relevant staff |

### Internal organisation (A.3)

| Strength | Example |
|---|---|
| Weak | A verbal statement that "someone is responsible for AI governance" |
| Better | An organisational chart showing an AI lead role |
| Strong | A role description with AI governance responsibilities listed, plus records of governance activities carried out by that person (e.g. meeting notes, sign-offs, decisions made) |

### Resources for AI systems (A.4)

| Strength | Example |
|---|---|
| Weak | A claim that staff are "trained on AI" |
| Better | A training course completion record |
| Strong | A training record linked to a specific role and AI system, showing date, content covered, and an assessment or acknowledgement of completion |

### Impact assessment (A.5)

| Strength | Example |
|---|---|
| Weak | "We consider impacts before deploying AI systems" |
| Better | An impact assessment template that has been used |
| Strong | A completed, dated impact assessment for a specific AI system, approved by the accountable person, with actions tracked and closed |

### AI system life cycle (A.6)

| Strength | Example |
|---|---|
| Weak | A general description of the development process |
| Better | A documented process covering design, testing, and deployment stages |
| Strong | Evidence that the process was followed for a specific system — e.g. test records, deployment approvals, and a post-deployment review |

### Data for AI systems (A.7)

| Strength | Example |
|---|---|
| Weak | "We use good quality data" |
| Better | A data quality policy |
| Strong | A data quality assessment for a specific dataset, showing what was checked, when, by whom, and what was decided |

### Information for interested parties (A.8)

| Strength | Example |
|---|---|
| Weak | "We tell people about our AI systems" |
| Better | Published information about AI use on the organisation's website |
| Strong | A communication plan showing what is disclosed, to whom, in what format, how often, and a record that communications have been made |

### Use of AI systems (A.9)

| Strength | Example |
|---|---|
| Weak | "We make sure AI is used appropriately" |
| Better | Acceptable use guidelines |
| Strong | Signed acceptable use acknowledgements from users, plus a record of how compliance is monitored and any exceptions handled |

### Third-party and customer relationships (A.10)

| Strength | Example |
|---|---|
| Weak | "Our contracts cover AI" |
| Better | A contract clause addressing AI-related obligations |
| Strong | A contract clause plus a supplier assessment record, a log of how obligations are monitored, and evidence of any issues raised and resolved |

## Common misunderstanding

Many organisations confuse quantity of documentation with quality of evidence. A large folder of policies that have never been reviewed, communicated, or acted on is weak evidence. A smaller set of current, owned, and actively used records is strong evidence.

## Suggested memory hook

**Currency. Specificity. Completeness. Ownership.**
Ask those four questions for every piece of evidence before the auditor does.

## Notes for visual treatment

**Professional card:** A three-column table showing Weak / Better / Strong evidence for three or four selected control areas. Clean, structured layout. Use colour coding: red or amber for weak, yellow for better, green for strong. Include a short header row explaining the four quality dimensions (Currency, Specificity, Completeness, Ownership).

**Funny card:** Three doors or filing cabinets labelled Weak, Better, and Strong. Behind the Weak door: a dusty folder with no date and a question mark. Behind the Better door: a tidy document with a date stamp. Behind the Strong door: a complete set of linked records with checkmarks, a calendar, a named owner badge, and a green approval stamp. An auditor character peers behind each door with progressively more satisfied expressions.
