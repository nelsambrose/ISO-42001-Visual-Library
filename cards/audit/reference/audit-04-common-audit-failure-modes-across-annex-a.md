# Common Audit Failure Modes Across Annex A

## Purpose

To identify the recurring patterns that weaken audit confidence across Annex A controls — helping practitioners recognise and address these issues before an audit rather than during one.

## Plain English explanation

Audit findings do not usually happen because an organisation has no controls. They happen because controls that exist are incomplete, unowned, outdated, or disconnected from actual practice. The same failure patterns appear repeatedly across organisations of different sizes and sectors.

Knowing the failure modes is the first step to avoiding them.

## Why it matters

Nonconformities and observations slow down certification, trigger follow-up audits, and create reputational risk. Most of them are preventable. Many stem from the same handful of root causes: lack of ownership, no review cycle, documentation that does not reflect practice, and evidence that cannot be found or linked to the control being assessed.

## Key points to cover

- Failure modes are usually systemic, not isolated
- The same weakness often affects multiple controls simultaneously
- Many failures stem from controls being set up but never maintained
- Good audit preparation means actively looking for these patterns before the auditor arrives

## Practical examples

### 1. Outdated documents

Policies, procedures, and assessments that have not been reviewed within their stated review period. A policy dated three years ago with no review record tells an auditor the control may not be actively managed.

**Fix:** Establish a review calendar. Record the outcome of every review — even if no changes are made, record that fact.

### 2. No named owner

A control exists on paper but no individual or role is accountable for it. When the auditor asks "who owns this?", the answer is vague or contested.

**Fix:** Every control must have a named owner or a named role. The owner must know they are the owner and be able to describe what they do.

### 3. No review trail

There is a process or policy, but there is no record of it ever being reviewed, tested, or assessed. The organisation claims it is reviewed regularly but cannot demonstrate it.

**Fix:** Record every review, even brief ones. A short entry in a log — date, who reviewed, outcome — is sufficient. Silence is not.

### 4. Evidence not linked to the control

A document or record exists, but it does not clearly relate to the specific control being assessed. For example, a general data governance policy presented as evidence for a specific AI data quality control.

**Fix:** When preparing evidence, map each record explicitly to the control it supports. Do not rely on the auditor to make the connection.

### 5. Inconsistent records

Different people or teams describe the same process differently, or records contradict each other. This signals to an auditor that the process is not standardised or consistently applied.

**Fix:** Align descriptions before the audit. If there are legitimate variations in practice, document them and explain the rationale.

### 6. Controls existing only on paper

The documented process does not reflect what actually happens. People follow a different approach in practice, or the documented process has never been implemented.

**Fix:** Close the gap between documentation and practice before the audit. Either update the document to reflect practice, or change practice to match the document.

### 7. Missing approvals

Documents or decisions lack the required sign-off. Policies without an approval date, impact assessments without a sign-off, or changes made without going through the stated change process.

**Fix:** Make approval a visible step in every process. Record who approved, when, and in what capacity.

### 8. Poor version control

Multiple versions of the same document in circulation, with no clear indication of which is current. Staff may be working from superseded guidance.

**Fix:** Use version numbers, effective dates, and a single controlled location for all current documents. Archive superseded versions clearly.

### 9. No evidence of communication or training

Controls require that relevant people know about them and understand them. If there is no record of communication or training, the auditor cannot confirm that people are operating the control as intended.

**Fix:** Keep distribution records, training completion logs, induction checklists, or meeting notes that show communication has taken place.

### 10. No follow-up on issues

Issues, findings, nonconformities, or incidents have been identified but there is no record of what was decided, what action was taken, or whether the action was effective.

**Fix:** Maintain a corrective action log. Record every issue, the root cause analysis, the action taken, the completion date, and the verification that the action was effective.

## Common misunderstanding

A common belief is that auditors are primarily looking for missing documents. In practice, missing follow-up on known issues is often more damaging. An organisation that identifies a problem and does nothing about it demonstrates a management system that does not drive improvement — which is a fundamental expectation of any ISO standard.

## Suggested memory hook

**Own it. Date it. Link it. Follow up on it.**
Those four habits eliminate most audit failure modes before they happen.

## Notes for visual treatment

**Professional card:** A numbered list of the ten failure modes with a one-line description and a corresponding fix for each. Use a two-column layout (Failure Mode / Fix). Include a brief header: "The ten patterns that cause most ISO 42001 audit findings." Clean, structured, suitable for workplace reference.

**Funny card:** A series of small illustrated vignettes, each showing a classic failure — a dusty folder with cobwebs (no review), a question mark over an empty chair (no owner), a stack of documents with conflicting version numbers (poor version control), and a rubbish bin with an unresolved issue inside (no follow-up). Each vignette has a short humorous caption. Title: "The greatest hits of audit failure — don't add your name to this list."
