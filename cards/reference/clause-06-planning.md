# ISO 42001 Reference Card: Clause 6 — Planning

---

## Purpose

Clause 6 is where the organisation plans how the AIMS will address AI-related risk, opportunity, objectives and change.

The clause connects the organisation's context from Clause 4 and leadership direction from Clause 5 to practical risk-based governance. It asks the organisation to decide what could go wrong, what needs to go right, what objectives it will pursue, and how changes to the AIMS will be controlled.

---

## Sub-clauses

### 6.1 Actions to address risks and opportunities

The organisation must plan actions to address risks and opportunities relevant to the AIMS.

This includes risks and opportunities arising from the organisation's context, interested party requirements, AI policy and intended outcomes of the AIMS.

**The organisation should plan how to:**
- Identify AI-related risks and opportunities
- Assess their significance
- Decide appropriate treatment actions
- Integrate those actions into AIMS processes
- Evaluate whether the actions are effective

**AI-related risks may include:**
- Harmful or discriminatory outcomes
- Inaccurate, unreliable or unsafe AI outputs
- Insufficient human oversight
- Poor data quality or inappropriate data use
- Lack of transparency or explainability
- Security vulnerabilities or misuse
- Regulatory non-compliance
- Reputational damage
- Excessive dependence on third-party AI systems

**AI-related opportunities may include:**
- Better customer outcomes
- Improved operational efficiency
- More consistent decision support
- Improved monitoring and assurance
- Better documentation and traceability
- Increased trust with customers, regulators and partners

Risk planning should be proportionate. A low-impact internal productivity tool does not need the same level of control as an AI system affecting access to employment, credit, health, safety or essential services.

---

### 6.1 and Annex A control selection

Planning must include determining which controls are needed to treat risks and meet requirements.

Annex A provides normative controls. The organisation must consider these controls and document whether each control is applicable or not, usually through a Statement of Applicability.

**Good control selection explains:**
- Which Annex A controls are applicable
- Why selected controls are needed
- Why excluded controls are not applicable
- How selected controls are implemented
- How implementation effectiveness is checked

The Statement of Applicability should be traceable to the risk assessment, interested party requirements, legal obligations and operational AI use cases.

---

### 6.2 AI objectives and planning to achieve them

The organisation must establish AI objectives at relevant functions and levels.

AI objectives translate policy commitments into measurable direction. They should be consistent with the AI policy, relevant to the AIMS, measurable where practicable, monitored, communicated and updated as needed.

**Examples of AI objectives:**
- Complete AI system inventory coverage for all in-scope business units
- Conduct AI risk assessments before deployment for all in-scope systems
- Establish defined approval gates for high-impact AI systems
- Complete role-based AI governance awareness training
- Monitor post-deployment performance and incidents for critical AI systems
- Reduce unresolved AI governance findings within a defined timeframe

**Planning should define:**

| Planning question | What to document |
|---|---|
| What will be done? | The objective and related activities |
| What resources are needed? | People, tools, budget and information |
| Who is responsible? | Accountable owners and supporting roles |
| When will it be completed? | Milestones and review points |
| How will results be evaluated? | Measures, evidence and success criteria |

Objectives should be meaningful. Audit evidence is stronger when objectives are tied to real governance outcomes rather than generic statements such as "use AI responsibly".

---

### 6.3 Planning of changes

Changes to the AIMS must be planned.

AI governance changes can introduce new risks if they are rushed, unclear or poorly communicated. Clause 6.3 requires changes to be made in a controlled way.

**Examples of changes that may need planning:**
- Expanding the AIMS scope to new AI systems or business units
- Changing AI approval processes or governance forums
- Introducing a new AI risk assessment methodology
- Changing control ownership
- Moving from internally developed AI to third-party AI services
- Adopting generative AI tools across the organisation
- Changing monitoring, incident or escalation processes

**Change planning should consider:**
- The purpose of the change
- Potential consequences
- Resource needs
- Impacts on roles and responsibilities
- Impacts on documented information
- Communication and training needs
- How effectiveness will be checked after implementation

---

## Common audit questions

- How does the organisation identify and assess AI-related risks and opportunities?
- How are Annex A controls selected, justified and documented?
- Where is the Statement of Applicability, and how is it kept current?
- What AI objectives have been set, and how are they measured?
- How are changes to the AIMS planned and controlled?
- Can risk treatment actions be traced through to operational controls?

## Key message

Clause 6 is the AIMS planning engine. It turns context, leadership direction and policy commitments into risk-based actions, objectives and controlled change.
