# ISO 42001 Reference Card: Clause 4 — Context of the Organisation

---

## Purpose

Clause 4 establishes the foundation of the AIMS. Before an organisation can govern AI, it must understand itself: its purpose, its environment, its stakeholders, and the boundaries within which the management system will operate.

Nothing in Clauses 5–10 can be properly designed without the groundwork of Clause 4.

---

## Sub-clauses

### 4.1 Understanding the organisation and its context

The organisation must identify **internal and external issues** that are relevant to its purpose and that affect its ability to achieve the intended outcomes of its AIMS.

**External issues include:**
- The regulatory environment (EU AI Act, national legislation, sector regulation)
- Market and competitive factors
- Social expectations and public perception of AI
- Technology trends and dependencies
- Relationships with AI system providers and suppliers

**AI-specific external considerations:**
- Which AI applications are subject to regulation in the jurisdictions where the organisation operates
- Public and stakeholder expectations regarding responsible AI use
- Emerging standards and guidance (ISO 23894, NIST AI RMF, etc.)

**Internal issues include:**
- Organisational values, culture and strategy
- Existing management systems (ISO 27001, ISO 9001)
- Current AI capabilities, maturity and practices
- Governance structures and decision-making authorities
- Resources, skills and competencies available

**AI-specific internal considerations:**
- The types of AI systems the organisation develops, deploys or uses
- The degree of AI integration into products, services and operations
- Existing AI policies, ethics principles or codes of conduct

---

### 4.2 Understanding the needs and expectations of interested parties

The organisation must identify **interested parties** relevant to its AIMS and understand their requirements.

**Who are interested parties?**

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Internal | Employees, management, board, developers |
| Customers | End users of AI-powered products or services |
| Suppliers | AI system providers, data providers, cloud providers |
| Regulators | National AI authorities, sector regulators, data protection authorities |
| Society | Communities affected by AI decisions, civil society organisations |
| Other | Shareholders, insurers, certification bodies |

**What requirements should be identified?**
- Legal and regulatory obligations (what must be done)
- Contractual commitments (what has been agreed)
- Stakeholder expectations (what is expected even if not mandated)
- Ethical principles (what the organisation has committed to)

The organisation must monitor and review this information, as requirements evolve. Regulatory environments are particularly volatile in AI.

---

### 4.3 Determining the scope of the AIMS

The organisation must determine the **boundaries and applicability** of the AIMS.

**Scope considerations:**
- Which AI systems are in scope (by product, service, function, geography)?
- Which parts of the organisation does the AIMS apply to?
- Are third-party AI systems in scope (where the org is a user, not developer)?
- Are AI systems under development as well as deployed systems in scope?

**Documenting the scope:**
The scope must be documented as **documented information**. It must be clear, unambiguous and available to interested parties.

**Scope must be consistent** with:
- The organisation's context (4.1)
- The requirements of interested parties (4.2)
- The organisation's actual AI activities

Artificially narrow scope — scoping out high-risk AI systems while claiming AIMS certification — is a common audit finding. Auditors will probe whether the scope genuinely reflects where AI risk exists.

---

### 4.4 AI management system

The organisation must **establish, implement, maintain and continually improve** an AIMS in accordance with the requirements of ISO 42001.

This is the formal commitment clause. It means the AIMS is not a one-time project but an ongoing system that must function, be maintained and improve over time.

**What "establish" means:**
Create the policies, processes, roles and documented information required by the standard.

**What "implement" means:**
Put the AIMS into actual operation. Documented processes must be followed in practice.

**What "maintain" means:**
Keep the AIMS current as the organisation's AI activities, risks and context change.

**What "continually improve" means:**
Drive ongoing enhancement of the AIMS's suitability, adequacy and effectiveness (see Clause 10).

---

## Common audit questions

- How has the organisation identified relevant external and internal issues?
- How often is this assessment reviewed?
- Who are the identified interested parties, and how were their requirements gathered?
- What is the documented scope of the AIMS, and why are certain systems or activities out of scope?
- Can the organisation demonstrate that the scope is consistent with its actual AI risk?
